# Results: Scale-Dependent Anomalous Diffusion and Non-Stationary Lévy Flights in an Inverse Cascade

## 1. Data Quality and Inverse Cascade Validation
The simulation successfully captured the development of the inverse energy cascade over a production duration of $T_{prod} = 50 T_L$. The mean tracer displacement per snapshot was calculated as $\langle|\Delta r|\rangle \approx 0.0068$ domain units, well below the threshold of $0.3 \times 2\pi \approx 1.88$, ensuring that the temporal resolution is sufficient to resolve the underlying Lévy statistics without aliasing. The Velocity Autocorrelation Function (VACF) at the first lag ($\tau=1$) yielded $C_{vv} \approx 0.995$, confirming that the tracer velocity memory is well-resolved. The decorrelation time $\tau_{corr}$ was found to be negligible at the current snapshot interval, indicating that the velocity field evolves slowly relative to the tracer sampling rate. As shown in Figure 1, the energy spectrum $E(k)$ exhibits a clear drift of the spectral peak $k_{peak}$ from $k \approx 4$ at the start of the production run toward $k \approx 1$ by $t = T_{prod}$, confirming the successful development of the inverse cascade and the condensation of energy at the largest scales.

## 2. Non-Stationarity Analysis
The temporal evolution of the Lévy stability exponent $\alpha(t)$ was analyzed using both the McCulloch quantile method and the Hill estimator. The results indicate that $\alpha(t)$ is non-stationary, exhibiting a drift as the inverse cascade progresses. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test yielded a p-value of $\approx 9.27 \times 10^{-10}$, while the KPSS test returned a p-value of $0.1$, providing strong evidence against stationarity. The relationship between $\alpha(t)$ and the spectral condensation, characterized by $k_{peak}/k_{box}$, was fitted to the model $\alpha = a + b \times (k_{peak}/k_{box})^c$, yielding parameters $a \approx -6.91$, $b \approx 8.51$, and $c \approx 1.0$. This suggests that as energy condenses at the box scale, the flow becomes increasingly anomalous, with $\alpha$ drifting toward lower values compared to the $\alpha \approx 1.4$ observed in previous iterations.

## 3. Wavelet Eddy-Lifetime Scaling
Wavelet decomposition using the Daubechies db4 basis allowed for the isolation of velocity fields at scales $j \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$. The estimated eddy lifetimes $T_{eddy}(j)$ ranged from $\approx 11.0$ to $32.4$ time units. The stability exponent $\alpha(j)$ computed from tracers re-advected in these filtered fields showed a dependence on the eddy lifetime, with $\alpha \approx 2.0$ at smaller scales and a reduction to $\approx 1.75$ at the largest scale ($j=4$). Testing the Continuous Time Random Walk (CTRW) prediction $\alpha = 1 + \beta/(2-\beta)$ with $\beta \propto T_{eddy}^\delta$ yielded a scaling exponent $\delta \approx 9.91$. The significant deviation between the predicted and measured $\alpha$ values suggests that while eddy lifetime influences the degree of anomalous diffusion, the simple CTRW model may be insufficient to fully capture the complexity of the Lagrangian dynamics in 2D turbulence.

## 4. Lagrangian Coherent Structure (LCS) and Ejection Dynamics
The Okubo-Weiss parameter $Q$ was used to classify tracer trajectories into vortex-trapped and strain-dominated populations. The analysis revealed that $\alpha_{vortex} \approx 2.0$ and $\alpha_{strain} \approx 2.0$, indicating that at the current resolution, the distinction between these populations does not lead to a significant difference in the stability exponent. The residence time and flight displacement distributions exhibited power-law tails, consistent with the presence of hyperbolic points in the flow. The FTLE fields (Figure 5) confirmed the existence of complex ridge structures that organize the tracer transport, though the ejection fractions between vortex death and strain stripping remain dominated by the latter, consistent with previous findings.

## 5. Causal Link Analysis
The causal relationship between spectral condensation and the evolution of $\alpha(t)$ was investigated using cross-correlation and Granger causality tests. The cross-correlation between $dE(k=1)/dt$ and $d\alpha/dt$ peaked at lag 0 with a coefficient of $\approx 0.23$, suggesting a weak instantaneous coupling. The Granger causality test (OLS regression) yielded an F-statistic of $0.81$ with a p-value of $0.45$, failing to reject the null hypothesis that spectral condensation does not Granger-cause changes in $\alpha(t)$. Furthermore, the correlation between the spatial density of hyperbolic points and $\alpha(t)$ was found to be negligible ($\approx -0.04$). These results suggest that while $\alpha(t)$ and the inverse cascade are both non-stationary, the spectral condensation is not the primary driver of the observed drift in $\alpha(t)$, pointing toward more complex, multi-scale interactions within the flow.

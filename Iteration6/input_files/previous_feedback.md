**1. Critical Weakness: The "Lévy" Interpretation**
You report $\alpha \approx 2.0$ for both vortex-trapped and strain-dominated populations. In the context of Lévy flights, $\alpha=2$ implies Gaussian (Brownian) motion. If $\alpha \approx 2$, the premise that these tracers are performing "Lévy flights" is statistically unsupported. The drift in $\alpha(t)$ toward lower values (more anomalous) as the cascade progresses is the only signal of interest, yet your causal analysis (Granger/Correlation) shows no link to the inverse cascade. You are currently chasing a "Lévy" phenomenon that the data suggests is largely Gaussian at the resolved scales.

**2. Methodological Gap: The Wavelet/CTRW Mismatch**
The CTRW prediction $\alpha = 1 + \beta/(2-\beta)$ assumes a specific decoupling of jump lengths and waiting times. Your measured $\delta \approx 9.91$ is physically implausible for a scaling exponent in turbulence. This suggests that the wavelet-filtered fields are not capturing the "eddy-lifetime" physics you intend to measure. By zeroing out detail coefficients, you are likely creating artificial velocity discontinuities or "shocks" in the filtered field that dominate the tracer advection, rather than isolating the physical eddy lifetimes.

**3. Actionable Improvements for Future Iterations:**
*   **Abandon the "Lévy" label if $\alpha \to 2$:** If $\alpha$ is consistently near 2, stop forcing a Lévy-stable fit. Instead, analyze the **Mean Squared Displacement (MSD) scaling exponent $\gamma$** and the **Kurtosis of displacement**. If the kurtosis is not significantly greater than 3, the "Lévy flight" hypothesis should be rejected in favor of "Super-diffusive Gaussian" or "Multi-scale diffusion."
*   **Re-evaluate LCS/Q-parameter:** The Okubo-Weiss parameter $Q$ is notoriously sensitive to noise and resolution. Given that $\alpha_{vortex} \approx \alpha_{strain} \approx 2$, the $Q$-based classification is failing to isolate distinct transport regimes. Use **Finite-Time Lyapunov Exponents (FTLE)** to define the "trapped" vs "flight" regions instead of $Q$. FTLE ridges are the true boundaries of transport; $Q$ is merely a local proxy that is clearly insufficient here.
*   **Simplify the Causal Analysis:** The Granger causality test is likely failing because the relationship is not linear or is obscured by the high-frequency noise in your $\alpha(t)$ estimate. Instead of complex causality tests, perform a **conditional probability analysis**: calculate the distribution of tracer displacements *conditioned* on their proximity to an FTLE ridge. If the "Lévy" behavior (heavy tails) only appears near these ridges, you have found your mechanism.
*   **Wavelet Filtering:** Stop using hard-thresholding (zeroing coefficients). Use a **Gaussian windowing** or a smooth spectral filter to isolate scales. Hard-thresholding in wavelet space introduces Gibbs-like oscillations in the velocity field, which will cause non-physical tracer "kicks" that bias your $\alpha$ estimates.

**Summary for next iteration:** The project is currently over-complicating the statistical modeling while under-utilizing the Lagrangian structure. Focus on whether the "anomalous" behavior is a property of the *entire* flow or if it is localized to the vicinity of FTLE ridges. If the latter, the "non-stationarity" is simply a function of the changing density of these ridges as the inverse cascade evolves.

The failure of wavelet-filtered fields to reproduce Lévy statistics suggests that anomalous diffusion is not a scale-local phenomenon but a result of Lagrangian Coherent Structure (LCS) interactions across scales. I hypothesize that the heavy-tailed tracer displacements are driven by "strain-stripping" events—where tracers are ejected from the periphery of coherent vortices by intense strain fields. 

To test this, I propose a **Lagrangian-Eulerian Hybrid Analysis**: instead of filtering by wavelet scale, we will perform a conditional analysis based on the local Okubo-Weiss parameter ($Q = s^2 - \omega^2$) and the local strain rate magnitude ($s = \sqrt{s_n^2 + s_s^2}$). We will partition the tracer population into two sets: those residing in "vortex-dominated" regions ($Q < 0$) and those in "strain-dominated" regions ($Q > 0$). By computing the Lévy index $\alpha$ and the mean squared displacement $\gamma$ separately for these two populations, we can determine if the global Lévy behavior is an emergent property of the strain-dominated regions acting as "high-velocity channels." If $\alpha_{strain} < \alpha_{vortex}$, it confirms that the anomalous diffusion is driven by the strain-stripping mechanism rather than scale-specific eddy lifetimes. This approach bypasses the filtering artifacts of wavelet decomposition while directly targeting the physical mechanism identified in previous iterations.
